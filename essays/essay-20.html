What "Sacred" Actually Meant

The word has been used to control people for so long that we forgot it once named something we are now starving for.
6 min read🎧 Audio coming soon

I want to rescue a word, carefully, because it has been so badly misused that most people flinch at it — and in flinching, miss something they are genuinely missing. The word is sacred, as in the idea that some things about a person are sacred. For a long time that word was a weapon: used to tell people, women especially, how to behave, what to cover, what to withhold, how to be "pure." That usage was controlling and it deserved to be rejected. But in throwing out the controlling meaning, we also lost a truer and gentler one — a meaning that has nothing to do with telling anyone how to live, and everything to do with a kind of privacy we are now starving for without knowing its name.

Let me be as clear as I can, up front, because this is the kind of idea that gets misheard: nothing here is about how anyone should dress, behave, or present themselves. This is not a prescription. It is the recovery of a word for something we lost.

What it never meant

First, the clearing-away, because the controlling meaning has to be set down before the real one can be picked up. "Sacred" has too often been code for a demand — be modest, be pure, withhold yourself, behave according to someone else's rules about what a good woman is. In that usage it was never really about the person it claimed to honour; it was about controlling her, dressing up a set of restrictions as reverence. That meaning is genuinely poisonous, and rejecting it was right. If "sacred" only meant that, we would be better off without the word entirely.

So whatever I mean by it, I do not mean that. I do not mean modesty, or purity, or any prescribed way of being. I am not telling anyone to withhold anything, cover anything, or perform any version of themselves. The controlling meaning is exactly what this essay is not about — and unless that is set down firmly at the start, everything that follows will be misheard as the very thing it rejects.

What it actually meant

Here is the meaning worth rescuing. To call something sacred, in the sense I mean, is simply to say it is not for public consumption — that it is diminished by being made totally visible, and kept whole by being kept private. It is the dignity of the unobserved: the parts of a self that are yours alone, not because they are shameful, but because they are yours, and exposure would thin them out.

Think of the difference between a thought you have only ever had alone and the same thought once you have posted it for reactions. Something changes in the second version — it becomes partly a performance, shaped by the imagined audience, no longer purely your own. The "sacred," in this sense, is whatever is diminished by that transition: the private interior, the unobserved experience, the part of you that exists for no audience. It is sacred not because a rule protects it, but because visibility consumes it — because some things are only fully themselves when no one is watching. This meaning prescribes nothing. It simply names a category of experience that total visibility is quietly destroying.

Why we are starving for it

And we are starving for it, precisely because the culture of total visibility has been consuming it without our noticing. We have been encouraged, relentlessly, to make everything visible — every thought shareable, every moment content, every part of the self available for public consumption. And in doing so we have been quietly eroding the category of the sacred-in-this-sense: the private interior that is diminished by exposure.

The result is a specific and widespread modern hunger that people feel but rarely name — a sense of being spread too thin, performed-out, never quite in possession of a self that is fully their own. That hunger is, I think, the felt absence of the sacred: the cost of having made nearly everything visible, leaving almost nothing held back, unobserved, purely one's own. We did not just lose some privacy in the practical sense. We lost the dignity of having an interior that belongs to no audience — and the loss registers as a low, sourceless depletion, the feeling of a self that has been entirely spent into visibility with nothing kept in reserve.

Reclaiming it, on your own terms

The recovery here is not a rule, and it is emphatically not a return to the controlling meaning. It is an invitation, available to anyone, on entirely their own terms: to keep something back. Not out of shame, not according to anyone's prescription about what should be kept, but as a deliberate reclaiming of the dignity of the unobserved.

It looks like deciding that some thoughts, some experiences, some parts of yourself are simply not for broadcast — not because they are too shameful to share, but because they are too yours to spend. It looks like protecting an interior that exists for no audience, recovering the category of the private-because-precious rather than the private-because-hidden. What you choose to keep sacred in this sense is entirely up to you; the point is not what you withhold but the recovery of the capacity to withhold anything at all — to have a self that is not, in its entirety, available for consumption.

The word "sacred" was stolen and turned into a cage, and rejecting the cage was right. But underneath the cage was something real and worth having: the dignity of an unobserved self, the part of you kept whole by being kept private. That was never a rule imposed from outside. It was a possession quietly taken from you, sold off one share at a time in exchange for visibility — and you are allowed, whenever you choose, to take it back. Not because anyone tells you to. Because it was always yours.
