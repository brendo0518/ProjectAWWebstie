What the Apps Optimise For

A dating app that reliably found you a partner would lose a customer. Hold that thought, and the whole experience starts to make sense.
7 min read🎧 Audio coming soon

If you have used a dating app and come away feeling slightly worse — about yourself, about the people on it, about the whole enterprise of meeting someone — I want to offer you a reframe that is not consoling but is, I think, clarifying. The experience was not a malfunction. The frustration was not a bug you could have avoided by writing a better profile or swiping more wisely. The experience is, in a structural sense, the product working as intended. Just not for you.

This sounds like conspiracy, and it is not. It is something duller and harder to escape: a misalignment of incentives so basic that no amount of good faith inside the company can fully fix it. Let me show you the misalignment, because once you see it, a great deal of otherwise demoralising experience stops feeling like a verdict on you.

The incentive sitting under the whole thing

Consider what a dating app is, as a business. It is mostly a subscription or an advertising product, which means its revenue depends on users continuing to use it. Now consider what success looks like for you: you meet someone, you pair off, and you delete the app. Your success is the company losing a customer.

Sit with that, because it is the whole essay in one sentence. The outcome you want — finding someone and leaving — is, from the platform's perspective, churn. A dating app that paired everyone off quickly and permanently would be a business that systematically destroyed its own user base. The app does not want you single forever, exactly, but it has no structural reason to want you partnered quickly, and every structural reason to keep you on the app, searching, engaged, subscribed, hopeful, and present.

I am not claiming a room of executives plotting to keep you lonely. I am claiming something more ordinary and more inescapable: the thing that makes you happy and the thing that makes the app money point in opposite directions, and over time, systems drift towards what makes them money. The app is optimised for the search, not the find — because the search is the product and the find is the exit.

What "optimised for the search" feels like from inside

Once you know what the system is optimised for, the texture of the experience decodes itself.

The endless feed of profiles, refreshing forever, framed as abundance — that is the search being made pleasurable enough to continue. The little hits of validation when someone matches, dispensed unpredictably — that is the engagement loop that keeps you opening the app the way you would a slot machine. The way everyone starts to feel slightly disposable, swipeable, replaceable by the next profile a thumb-flick away — that is what happens when human beings are presented as an infinite scrollable supply. The sense that there is always someone potentially better one more swipe along — that is not your shallowness; it is the interface teaching you to feel it, because a person who believes the next swipe might be better is a person who keeps swiping.

None of these is your failure of character. They are the predictable felt experience of a system designed to keep you in the search. The disposability, the restlessness, the low-grade dissatisfaction that follows you off the app and into actual dates — these are the search economy doing to your psychology exactly what it is built to do.

The cruelty of abundance

Here is the part that genuinely surprises people, because it runs against intuition. You would think more options would make finding a partner easier. The opposite is closer to true, and there is decent psychology behind it.

Beyond a fairly low number, more choice does not liberate; it paralyses and devalues. When you have three plausible options, you weigh them and choose. When you have three thousand, presented as an endless renewable supply, no single one feels worth committing to, because commitment means foreclosing all the others, and the others feel infinite. Abundance, past a point, does not enrich the search. It starves it — of the very capacity to choose and stay chosen that the whole exercise was supposed to serve. The app gives you more people than a human being can meaningfully evaluate and, in doing so, makes each individual person feel like less.

So the abundance that the app presents as its great gift is, in practice, part of what keeps the find out of reach. You are not failing to find someone despite all the options. You are partly failing to find someone because of them.

Using the tool without being used by it

I am not going to tell you to delete every app and meet people exclusively in libraries and on hiking trails. For a great many people the apps are now simply where the people are, and the lofty advice to abandon them is easy to give and hard to live.

But you can use the tool differently once you know what it is optimised for. You can treat it as a way to get off the app as fast as possible — to move to an actual meeting quickly, where a real person can become real rather than remaining one more profile in an infinite feed. You can refuse the framing of abundance, deliberately, by not treating people as a renewable supply, by giving an actual human being the kind of attention the interface is designed to prevent. You can notice the slot-machine pull and decline it. And you can hold, against everything the design is whispering, the truth that the next swipe is not better — that "better" is the carrot on the stick that keeps the search going, and that connection lives in the opposite direction, in the choosing and the staying that the app has no incentive to help you reach.

The apps are optimised for the search. You are allowed to be optimised for the find. Knowing the difference is most of what it takes to stop the tool from quietly working against the exact thing you opened it to get.
