Why "I Don't Need a Man" Became Mandatory

The strange thing about modern independence is not that people declare it. It is that they are no longer allowed not to.
6 min read🎧 Audio coming soon

There is a declaration that has become almost compulsory in certain corners of the culture, recited in captions and lyrics and bios with the regularity of a loyalty oath: I don't need a man. Or, in its mirror version aimed at men: I don't need a woman, I'm focused on myself, I don't chase, I provide for me. On the surface these are statements of strength and self-sufficiency. What is strange about them — and worth examining — is not that people say them. It is that, increasingly, people are not permitted not to.

The independence has become a mandate. And a mandate is a very different thing from a freedom, even when it wears freedom's exact words.

The difference between a freedom and a mandate

A freedom expands what you are allowed to be. The hard-won freedom to not need a partner — to build a whole, funded, respected life on your own terms — is real and good, and for most of history most people, women especially, did not have it. Nobody should want to give that back.

But somewhere the freedom curdled into its opposite shape. You are allowed to not need anyone became you are not allowed to need anyone. The option hardened into an obligation. And now the person who would actually like a partner — who feels the ordinary human wish to be chosen, held, depended on — finds that admitting it has become faintly shameful, a confession of weakness, a betrayal of the independence they are supposed to perform. The culture that once forbade them from leaving now forbids them from wanting to stay. The cage changed shape; it did not disappear.

This is the tell that something has gone wrong. A genuine freedom would leave room for its opposite — you could declare your independence or admit your longing, as you actually felt. A mandate permits only one answer. And "I don't need anyone" now functions, for a great many people, as the only socially acceptable answer, which means it has stopped being a free expression of how they feel and become a required performance of how they are supposed to feel.

What the mandate costs

The cost is paid in the gap between what people perform and what they actually feel, and that gap is where a particular modern loneliness lives.

Because the wanting does not go away when it is forbidden. The wish for connection is not a cultural construct you can affirmation your way out of; it is close to the centre of what human beings are. So the mandate does not abolish the longing. It just drives it underground, makes it undisclosable, forces people to broadcast a self-sufficiency they do not feel while privately wishing for exactly the connection they are publicly disowning. And then — this is the cruel part — they look around at everyone else performing the same mandated independence and conclude that they alone are secretly longing, that everyone else has genuinely transcended the need, that their own wish to be loved is a personal failing rather than a universal human condition that the whole crowd is busy hiding.

It is a hall of mirrors. Everyone performing not-needing at everyone else, each one privately needing, each one convinced they are the only one faking it. The mandate manufactures a loneliness and then convinces each lonely person that their loneliness is uniquely theirs.

Who benefits from the mandate

It is worth asking why "I don't need anyone" got amplified into a commandment, because the answer points, as usual, away from individual people and towards the systems around them.

A person who needs no one is the ideal unit of a consumer economy — self-contained, reachable through products rather than people, their needs met by things that can be sold rather than relationships that cannot. The platform also profits: the performance of independence is engaging content, it earns the affirming comments, it photographs well, while the quiet admission of longing does not perform and so is quietly unrewarded into silence. And the broader discourse, which runs on the war between the sexes, finds the mandate useful in both directions — I don't need a man and I don't need a woman are both serviceable ammunition, each performing defiance at the other side. The mandate serves the market, the platform, and the war. The one party it does not serve is the actual human being, required to disown a need they still feel.

Permission to want

The way out is not to swing back and mandate neediness instead — to make longing compulsory the way independence became compulsory. It is simply to restore the freedom the mandate replaced: the permission to feel however you actually feel, and to say so.

It is the permission to be genuinely self-sufficient and to want a partner, with no contradiction between them — because there is none. It is the recognition that wanting connection is not a failure of independence but a feature of being human, and that the strongest position is not "I need no one" but "I could manage alone, and I would rather not, and that is allowed." It is the small, quiet refusal to perform a transcendence you have not achieved and do not actually want.

"I don't need a man" — or a woman — stopped being a liberation the moment it became the only thing you were permitted to say. The genuine freedom underneath it was always larger and kinder than the slogan: you are allowed to need people, allowed to want to be chosen, allowed to feel the oldest human wish there is without filing it as a weakness. The mandate took that permission away and sold the theft back to you as strength. You are allowed to take the permission back. Wanting someone was never the failure. Being forbidden to admit it was.
